Package Center: A Closer Look

One of the things that brings Columbia students closer together is complaining. And each September, there’s nothing students complain about more than the Columbia Package Center.

From long lines—one student called the the corridor on the fourth floor of Lerner Hall a place “where time curves back onto itself”—to delayed deliveries, the Package Center has its fair share of issues. While the Package Center has done much to respond to these issues in recent years—including adding satellite centers, making use of kiosks, and adjusting hours—few students know what goes on inside the Package Center.

Spectator sat down with Miguel Pagan, executive director of administrative services, to discuss the challenges faced by Mail Services, and followed a single package on its path from the delivery truck to the Package Center counter. Spectator also requested data from recent years to examine the major trends and determine the best way to avoid the Package Center lines.

The Journey of the Package

After being unloaded from its delivery truck, a package enters Lerner Hall through the 114th Street entrance. The package is carted through Lerner’s lower level to a service elevator, which is ridden up to the fourth floor. The elevator opens up to the Package Center, where the package is processed before being stored on a shelf in the 1,255-square-foot space, awaiting pickup. In 2013, an average of 511 packages* went through this process each day.


*This average is approximated by dividing the total number of packages received in 2013 (127,730) by 250 days. The denominator is 365 days minus weekends and holidays. The Package Center staff does work some weekends, so the real average is slightly lower than 511.

Rush Period

The number of packages received* in August and September increased every year from 2005 to 2012, with a huge spike occurring between 2010 and 2011. Last year, the Package Center projected that it would receive 47,743 packages during the two-month period in 2013, but it only received 31,672, close to the 2010 number. This year saw a small rise to 32,528 (as of Sept. 28, 2014).

While the Package Center historically defined August to September as the “rush period” for purposes of Spectator information requests, a campus services spokesperson said the true period of highest volume is slightly different.

“Now we are seeing that October volume is steadily higher than August, so we know the true ‘rush period’ is really only the last week of August through the second week of October,” the spokesperson said.


*For the purposes of this piece, a "package" refers to any piece of mail that does not fit into student mailboxes and so must be treated like a package.

Monthly Count

Over the last four years, the pattern of how many packages are received each month has remained relatively consistent. Each year, there is a large spike in September coinciding with move-in and the start of the academic year. This spike was highest in 2012 and 2011, as shown in the previous graph. The number of packages received in the subsequent months dips back down to around 10,000 until there is a small jump in January and February coinciding with the start of the spring semester. From there, monthly numbers decline, hitting a low in July before jumping back up in August.

While 2013 did not show as big of a spike in packages received during August and September, there was a slightly higher volume in October than in previous years. However, from 2012 to 2013, there was still a net decrease in the number of packages received in the three-month period of August to October, from 56,863 to 47,260.

Pickup: 2013

At the start of the 2013 school year, the busiest week of students picking up packages from the Package Center was from Sept. 9 to Sept. 13, when 7,823 packages were distributed to students.

The yellow bar marks the first day of the New Student Orientation Program (Aug. 26), the green bar marks the first day of classes (Sept. 3), and the pink bars mark weekend days when the Package Center was open. As is visible in the graph, the Package Center was closed all other weekends and on Labor Day, Monday, Sept. 2. Though the extra weekend days were made available, the volume of packages picked up by students on those days was considerably lower than on week days.

Pickup: 2014

The start of the 2014 school year showed a slightly later spike in the number of packages picked up than the previous year.

As in the previous graph, the yellow bar marks the start of NSOP (Aug. 25), the green bar marks the first day of classes (Sept. 2), and the pink bars mark the weekend days when the Package Center was open. The busiest week was from Sept. 15 to Sept. 19—during those five days, 8,408 packages were distributed to students. But despite the fact that this peak came later in September than in 2013, there was still a drop off in the number of packages picked up at the end of the fourth week in September.

Also of note, for three of the four weeks in September, the first day of the week was the busiest, and like in the previous year, though the Package Center was open on the second weekend after the start of classes—the website shows it was open from 9 a.m. to 3 p.m. on both Saturday, Sept. 13 and Sunday, Sept. 14—a much smaller volume of packages was distributed on those days.
